About the project

A kiosk for trading follows
without trading data.

01 The premise

At conferences, fairs, and tabling events, the moment of "let's connect" usually collapses into fumbling — typing handles into someone else's phone, scanning a QR code in poor light, or trading a paper card that will be forgotten by Tuesday.

This is a small kiosk that lives on a table at these events. A visitor walks up. They tell the operator which platform they want to share — Instagram, Bluesky, LinkedIn, or a custom URL — and type their handle. A few seconds later, an NFC-enabled keychain on the counter is written with their profile URL. They hand it to a new contact. That person taps it to their phone. The profile opens. They follow.

It's the universality of tap-to-pay, applied to the small social ritual of "follow me."

Why a dedicated device?

A web app could in theory write NFC tags from a phone, but Web NFC is spotty across iOS, requires installs and permissions, and pushes the cognitive load onto the visitor. A small physical kiosk, run by a present operator, lets the visitor focus on a single decision — which profile do you want to share? — and walk away with a keychain that just works.

02 Design values

Privacy is architectural, not a setting.

The project's commitments are baked into the system, not toggled in a menu. Three rules drive the design:

  • Nothing is logged. Handles and URLs live in memory just long enough to write a tag, then are discarded. No file, no database, no telemetry.
  • No internet dependency. The kiosk does not phone home. It does not need the venue Wi-Fi. It works in a basement.
  • Walk-up usable. A visitor with no instructions should still get to a working tag. The interface assumes a present operator, but tries not to need one.

A future Business Order Mode is sketched into the architecture for paid commercial jobs (locked tags, bulk writes, optional logging with tag UIDs) — but it requires explicit operator opt-in. The default path stays private.

03 The build

From breadboard to kiosk.

It began on an Arduino with a 16×2 character LCD. It moved through several platform iterations as the requirements grew — on-device platform list, Unicode handles, screen-based UI — and now runs on a Raspberry Pi 4 with a small HDMI display in Chromium kiosk mode. A Flask + SocketIO backend talks to a USB PN532 NFC reader and listens to any standard USB keyboard for input.
